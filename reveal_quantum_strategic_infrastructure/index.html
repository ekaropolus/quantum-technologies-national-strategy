Quantum Technologies as Strategic Infrastructure Edgar Valdés · Hadox Research Labs
Opening title slide. Introduce the talk as a strategic infrastructure argument, not a hardware-forecast presentation.
Distinguish the first quantum revolution, based on bulk and statistical quantum phenomena, from the second quantum revolution, based on direct engineering of quantum states.
Use this slide to anchor the empirical record: public commitments, public R&D awards, patent families, geographic concentration, and literature expansion.
The adoption paradox: organizations expect disruption but lack readiness planning, budgets, and validated use cases.
Emphasize absorptive capacity: knowledge enters institutions through organizational filters. Raw hardware capability cannot bypass those filters.
Separate adoption timelines: PQC is immediate, sensing is nearer term, quantum communication is specialized infrastructure, and quantum computing remains high optionality.
Explain the Mosca-style time problem: data shelf life plus migration time can exceed the time to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer.
Shift evaluation from physical qubits to logical-qubit economics. Hardware modalities represent different engineering bargains.
The central HPC argument: quantum computing enters as a specialized accelerator inside classical scientific workflows, not as a general replacement for supercomputing.
Application relevance is not adoption. Chemistry, optimization, finance, and machine learning must be evaluated against classical baselines and operational workflows.
Use the seven pillars to argue that readiness is institutional and operational, not merely scientific.
Present the roadmap as staged capability formation: preparation and security, testbeds and pilots, selective scaling.
These are the mistakes the paper asks policy scholars and decision-makers to avoid: waiting, hardware nationalism, confusing QKD with PQC, neutralizing QCaaS, baseless advantage claims, and underfunding the missing middle.
Close with the governing thesis: quantum readiness is cultivated institutional capability, not a hardware purchase.
